Theory & Discussion

A rigorous treatment of polynomial interpolation theory, comparing methods, deriving error bounds, and discussing numerical pitfalls such as Runge’s phenomenon.

⇌ Newton vs. Lagrange Interpolation

Both formulations find the unique interpolating polynomial of degree \(\leq n-1\) through \(n\) given points. Their algebraic structures differ, which leads to important practical distinctions.

Lagrange Form
\[P_{n-1}(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}y_i\prod_{\substack{j=0\\j\ne i}}^{n-1}\frac{x-x_j}{x_i-x_j}\]

Elegant and symmetric, but the basis polynomials \(L_i(x)\) must be entirely recomputed whenever a data point is added or removed.

Newton’s Divided Difference Form
\[P_{n-1}(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}f[x_0,\ldots,x_k]\,\omega_k(x),\quad \omega_k(x)=\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}(x-x_j)\]

Adding a new point \((x_n, y_n)\) only requires computing \(f[x_0,\ldots,x_n]\) and appending one term — all existing coefficients are unchanged.

∂ Derivation of Coefficients

Assume the interpolating polynomial has the nested (Horner-like) Newton form:

\[P_{n-1}(x)=c_0+c_1(x-x_0)+c_2(x-x_0)(x-x_1)+\cdots+c_{n-1}\prod_{j=0}^{n-2}(x-x_j)\]

Substituting the data points successively:

  • \(P(x_0)=c_0=y_0 \;\Rightarrow\; c_0=f[x_0]\)
  • \(P(x_1)=c_0+c_1(x_1-x_0)=y_1 \;\Rightarrow\; c_1=\dfrac{y_1-y_0}{x_1-x_0}=f[x_0,x_1]\)
  • By mathematical induction: \(c_k=f[x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_k]\)

The divided differences satisfy the recurrence, filling the table diagonally from left to right, each entry depending only on two adjacent entries from the previous column.

⚠ Interpolation Error & Runge’s Phenomenon

Theoretical Error Bound

If \(f\in C^n[a,b]\), the error at any point \(x\in[a,b]\) is:

\[E(x)=f(x)-P_{n-1}(x)=\frac{f^{(n)}(\xi)}{n!}\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(x-x_i)\]

where \(\xi\) is some unknown value in the smallest interval containing \(x\) and all the nodes. The error depends on both the \(n\)-th derivative of \(f\) and the product \(\omega_n(x)=\prod(x-x_i)\).

Runge’s Phenomenon
Using equally-spaced nodes and increasing \(n\) does not always converge. For Runge’s function: \[f(x)=\frac{1}{1+25x^2},\quad x\in[-1,1]\] high-degree polynomial interpolation produces large oscillations near the endpoints, even though the function is smooth.
